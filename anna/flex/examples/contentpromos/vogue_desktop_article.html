How The Chainsmokers Landed the Number One Hit in the Country

September 12, 2016 3:18 pm by Elias Leight
Expand

Photo: Frank Apollonio

  • email

Last year, Andrew Taggart and Alex Pall, who record as The Chainsmokers, were facing a songwriting conundrum. “We’re just two average, middle-class white guys,” Pall remembers thinking. “What’s our message to the world? What are we gonna say that brings people in and makes them feel something?”

They seem to have found their answer: The Chainsmokers have quietly become one of the most successful pop acts of 2016 with a hat trick of top 10 hits. Their latest, “Closer,” a duet between Taggart and the singer Halsey, has been number one in the country for three consecutive weeks. It’s not an immediately overwhelming track—Taggart is a competent, if not adept singer; you’ll hear any number of songs that use the same production palette. But none of that matters when the hook arrives, a glorious stockpile of syllables that spills giddily over the beat. The goofy assonance—“that Blink-182 song that we beat to death in Tucson”—evokes Nashville writing rooms, which still prize these clever turns of phrase.

The Chainsmokers didn’t originally form to make hits; they were more focused on making rent. Living in New York and pursuing music on the side in 2012, each member was “making ends meet, but barely,” according to Pall. “Our manager was like, ‘Maybe if you guys meet and hit it off, you’ll both bring something to the table that the other person is looking for.’”

They turned out to be well matched. “Drew [Taggart] is an incredible producer, and that was something I was really interested in learning and getting involved in,” Pall explains. “I had tons of DJ gigs around the city. So it was like, I got the shows, you’ve got the production side of things, let’s teach each other.”

To their surprise, the duo cracked the Billboard top 20 in 2014 with “#Selfie,” an irksome novelty track. “That was a crazy anomaly,” Pall says, comparing it to the popular “Damn, Daniel” meme. “It was meant just for our friends—next thing you know you’re on The Ellen DeGeneres Show.”

Expand

Photo: Frank Apollonio and Dash Grey

“#Selfie” cruises along at the tempo of house music and uses the synth textures common in Vegas-friendly electronic music. For this reason, The Chainsmokers are identified as a part of this scene, and participate in its major American rituals—they played EDC Las Vegas this year, and Electric Zoo in New York City earlier this month. That’s either a mischaracterization of the group, or a statement about how much pop’s mainstream and electronic music’s mainstream, once wildly separate, now overlap. Either way, looking back at “#Selfie,” Pall suggests it “was never really indicative of our sound.”

When The Chainsmokers returned to the charts a few years later with “Roses,” listeners could have easily mistaken the single for the work of another group. There were two major changes. The first was sonic: While “#Selfie” bounded off the walls like a middle schooler on a sugar high, “Roses” dawdled along, full of cushiony synths and willowy vocals from the singer Rozes. The other shift was thematic—the group focused on romance instead of social trends. The next hit, “Don’t Let Me Down,” followed suit, confirming the new direction.

Pall attributes the change in approach to two groups of artists that are rarely mentioned in the same breath. “We admire guys like Bon Iver, James Blake, and Chris Martin,” he says. “There’s a very personal feeling that you attribute to all of their music.” At the same time, The Chainsmokers wanted to take a page from Blink-182, Dashboard Confessional, and Taking Back Sunday, bands that “said exactly what they were feeling, instead of using metaphors about wings spreading and making weird analogies.”

It was the second collection of acts in particular that inspired “Closer” during a tour in the fall of 2015. “We were drunk after a show, so we were like, ‘Let’s listen to all the stuff we grew up on,” Pall remembers. While enjoying Blink-182 and co., The Chainsmokers had a boozy epiphany: Why don’t we write a song like this tomorrow?

Expand

Photo: Steve Rosenfield

At the time, Pall and Taggart were on the road with Louis the Child and Shaun Frank, both of whom played a role in the making of “Closer.” “I think it was in Urbana Champaign-Urbana [Illinois], we were just hanging out in the greenroom,” Louis the Child’s Freddy Kennett remembers in a separate phone conversation. “[Taggart] was like, ‘Yo, do you want to go hang in the bus and make music?’” Together, the two men came up with “the drop”—the metallic, hammering progression that follows “Closer”’s word jumble of a hook—and some other instrumental parts. “We were just playing around on the computer,” Kennett says. He didn’t hear the completed song until The Chainsmokers played it at Coachella months later.

Exit Kennett; fast-forward to the next day; enter Frank. “We were fucking hungover,” Frank says, speaking backstage before a recent performance at Electric Zoo. Despite this impediment, Frank remembers “pacing up and down on the bus. We knew that it was gonna be a dope idea to have those lyrics roll through the whole chorus and just keep going, and the rhyme structure could keep switching, kind of like an Eminem song. It was like, ‘What’s a weird word that rhymes here?’” Frank also convinced Taggart, who rarely sang lead on previous Chainsmokers records, that he could provide the vocal on this one.

The final piece of “Closer” came months later, when the duo enlisted Halsey—“In our opinion, she’s the best artist out there right now”—to play Taggart’s counterpart. “Since the song is about a relationship, maybe it would be interesting to tell both tales,” Pall remembers thinking. “With Drew it was a confession. When Halsey came into the song, it was a conversation.”

Now that the duo has found millions of new listeners, they face a new set of concerns. Right now, they’re wondering whether the fans who loved the hits will stay on board for an album. “It would suck to put out 12 songs and have people be like, ‘What’s the single?’” Pall says. “We don’t want to do it if our fans don’t want it. I don’t know what kind of metric we’ll use to address that.” But that’s not a bad problem for two average guys to have.
