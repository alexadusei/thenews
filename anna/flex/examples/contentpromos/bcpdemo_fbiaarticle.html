Kick CPM to the curb: Cost-per-view is the future of branded content pricing

May 24, 2016 May 24, 2016
Polar

When researching our latest customer-exclusive whitepaper, Branded Content, we interviewed over 30 premium publisher chief revenue officers and executives. We examined how they run, manage and sell their branded content programs.

The first area we explored was pricing and packaging - and when it comes to rate cards for branded content their is certainly a feeling that it's a bit of the 'wild-west.' Publishers struggle with how to consistently price and package branded content programs.

We first asked who's the buyer of branded content programs? The majority of publishers sell to combination of media agencies and brands directly.

Key findings:

  • Publisher sales teams are still more comfortable working with media agencies.
  • Brands still turn to agencies first as opposed to publishers for executing content marketing.
  • Brands are still very much experimenting with branded content and require RFP support.
  • Local and trade publishers rely mostly on brand-direct sales rather than agencies.

As we continue to see the native marketplace expand, we are seeing new groups of professionals taking the lead on these programs. While we initially saw most of the buying coming from media agencies, we are increasingly seeing new groups of clients coming from PR, communications, and in-house newsroom. -Pete Spande, Chief Revenue Officer, Business Insider

Next we asked, how do you price your branded content packages?

Above image: Cost-per-view (CPV) is the fastest growing pricing scheme with almost 50 percent of publishers pricing branded content based on views.

Most publishers currently offer a flexible pricing approach to branded content programs, with many providing a combination of alternatives. Today, 57 percent of publishers still offer CPM based pricing arrangement, which is most commonly used when dealing directly with media agencies.

This is still a holdover from media agencies that are more comfortable with legacy digital advertising with budgets usually tied to impressions and reach.

"CPMs for native media have been the primary pricing model for us due to agency budgets [being] structured this way, although program success is most commonly measured as views to the promoted content." - Greg Giusti, VP of Ad Products and Operations, Seeking Alpha

Polar's research predicts CPM-based pricing will likely be phased out in mature markets as content programs are increasingly measured on views and engagement with the branded content pages themselves in this era of brand recognition and social reach.

"As customers look to branded content for greater engagement, moving to a CPV model allows use to use social and other platforms to provide a more measurable experience," Liz Percy-Robb, Product Manager, Johnston Press.

The CPM rates published in the chart generally represent turnkey costs, which include: content production costs, paid social distribution, display promotional costs, and of course, native promotional media. Content and video views (or CPV) of branded content have become the new currency for sponsored programs. Many consumer publishers provide clients with hard-guarantees or soft guidance when pricing based on views.

As native advertising and branded content has matured - so too has pricing. Most of the CPM rates you see in the charts above are blended and don't truly account for many facets of a publisher's branded content program save for the actual inventory.

Instead integral parts of a comprehensive branded content campaign are lumped in with the initial inventory or not accounted for at all. These elements include:

  • Content production costs
  • Paid social distribution
  • Display media on branded content pages

Again, agencies prefer this method if only because that's how they have presented their costs to their clients in the first place.

If you're not already pricing based on CPV then your client's are likely calculating anyway. Polar recommends publishers shift to CPV model, which more accurately reflects the engagement of branded content. Regardless of how it's distributed, it incorporates views on whichever platform the content lives and still provides clients with a more accurate metric.

The data also begs the question: why are video views priced so much lower than other content views? The answer is supply and social platform such as Facebook, Snapchat, Twitter, Instagram are the culprits:

  • They are battling it out amongst themselves and the publishing industry to cement their place as they first destination for branded videos.
  • They flood the market with video inventory, driving down prices.
  • They drive viewers away from content creators as they content is all viewed offsite.

To achieve the breadth of execution which is expected of them, publishers need to utilize these social networks for the distribution of their video content - but would be wise to incorporate video into an all encompassing branded content strategy that keeps readers on their site or leads them to the brand's call-to-action, rather than relying on video alone.
